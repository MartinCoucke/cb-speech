MPC member Alan Taylor delivers a detailed speech on the role of the Bank of England's central forecast in monetary policy, examining its past limitations, current de-emphasis in favor of scenarios, and future reform priorities. He argues that despite the April 2026 MPR publishing only scenarios rather than a central forecast, the baseline projection remains essential as the anchor from which all scenarios are derived.   • “The result has been a persistent one-sided bias in our forecast errors from February 2025 up to February 2026... outturns were coming in cumulatively wrong to the downside – not necessarily because the MPC misread the economy, but, in part, because the framework and conditioning assumptions we had employed boxed in our analysis.”
  • “Scenarios are a complement not a substitute for the central forecast. They enrich the narrative, illuminate risks, and help the MPC communicate, especially in times of great uncertainty. But they cannot carry the weight of the entire forecasting system.”

Unknown speaker — Reserve Bank of Australia
Speech: “The Straight Line Belongs to Man, the Curved Line Belongs to God” (2026-06-24)
RBA Deputy Governor Andrew Hauser delivers a technically detailed lecture on the nonlinear Phillips curve, arguing that inflation pressures rise disproportionately when an economy is already near full capacity. He uses this framework to justify the RBA's recent rate hikes in February, March, and May 2026, while noting that credible disinflation on the steep part of the curve need not impose large unemployment costs. He acknowledges ongoing uncertainty and cautions against over-reliance on any single model.
The speech endorses a proactive tightening bias, explicitly validating three consecutive rate hikes and warning that being on the steep part of the Phillips curve means inflationary shocks can escalate quickly—implying a preference for pre-emptive action over gradualism. However, the hawkishness is tempered by acknowledgement of uncertainties, hysteresis risks, and the possibility that Middle East conflict resolution could ease pressures.
  • “In general, the more nonlinear the Phillips curve is, the stronger is the case for central banks who believe they are on the steeper part of the curve to take pro-active policy action to reduce excessive capacity pressures.”
  • “We still have work to do to reduce inflation here in Australia, which remains far too high.”
